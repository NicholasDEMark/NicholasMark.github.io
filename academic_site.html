My research sits at the intersection of twentieth-century intellectual history and the politics of knowledge production. I am interested in how ideas about expertise, objectivity, and authority have been contested across the humanities and social sciences—and what those contests reveal about the deeper fault lines of modern life.

My first book, The Uncertain Archive (University of Westbrook Press, 2021), examined how mid-century sociologists navigated the divide between empirical rigour and normative commitment. It received the Braudel Prize for outstanding first monograph in historical sociology.

I am currently completing a second book, tentatively titled Credibility and Its Discontents, which traces how the concept of the expert witness moved from courtrooms into public discourse between 1945 and 1985. I hold a DPhil from the University of Oxford and a BA from Oberlin College.
